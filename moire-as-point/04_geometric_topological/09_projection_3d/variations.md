# Variations

## 1. Multi-View Stereo Moiré
Use two projection angles (left and right "eyes"). The moiré creates *stereoscopic* depth — the fringe patterns for each eye differ, and the brain fuses them into 3D perception.

## 2. Time-of-Flight Moiré
Animate the projection angle over time. The fringes "walk" across the surface. The speed of fringe movement encodes surface slope — steeper = faster fringe travel.

## 3. Phase-Shifting Moiré
Shift the grid phase by known amounts (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°). Combine the four images to extract *unwrapped* depth — no fringe ambiguity. This is how industrial 3D scanners work.

## 4. Curved Reference Grid
Instead of a flat reference, use a cylindrical or spherical grid. The moiré contours follow the reference curvature — creates "warped" topographic maps.

## 5. Dynamic Surface
The surface itself animates (waves, fluid, cloth). The moiré contours *chase* the surface motion — like watching water level lines on a moving sea.

## 6. Inverse Moiré (Sculpture from Contours)
Instead of generating moiré from a known surface, *design* the moiré pattern and solve for the surface that produces it. The moiré becomes a *design tool* — specify the contours, derive the 3D form.
