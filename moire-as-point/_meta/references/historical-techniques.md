# Historical Techniques & Predecessors

## The Origin of "Moire"

### Moiré Silk
- **Era:** 18th century
- **Technique:** Watered silk creates iridescent patterns through woven thread interference
- **Significance:** The origin of the term "moiré" — from the French word for "watered."

### Moiré Taffeta
- **Era:** 19th century
- **Technique:** Ribbed fabric pressed to create wave-like patterns
- **Significance:** The textile ancestor of all moiré art.

## Early Optical Devices

### Praxinoscope
- **Inventor:** Émile Reynaud (1877)
- **Technique:** Early animation device using mirrors and strips
- **Significance:** Predecessor to kinegram logic — motion from static frames.

### Zoetrope
- **Inventor:** William George Horner (1834)
- **Technique:** Cylinder with slits and sequential images
- **Significance:** The physical kinegram — barrier grid animation in 3D form.

### Phenakistiscope
- **Inventor:** Joseph Plateau (1832)
- **Technique:** Spinning disk with radial slits
- **Significance:** The earliest motion picture device — stroboscopic animation.

### Thaumatrope
- **Inventor:** John Ayrton Paris (1824)
- **Technique:** Disk with images on both sides, spun to merge them
- **Significance:** The persistence-of-vision principle — the neurological foundation of all animation.

## Photography & Motion

### Eadweard Muybridge
- **Work:** Motion photography (1878)
- **Significance:** The content that kinegrams would later animate.

### Chronophotography
- **Pioneers:** Étienne-Jules Marey, Ottomar Anschütz
- **Significance:** Sequential photography as art and science — the ancestor of frame-based moiré.

## Print & Halftone

### Benjamin Day
- **Era:** 19th century
- **Technique:** Ben-Day dots — uniform dots for shading in printing
- **Significance:** The origin of halftone dot patterns.

### Halftone Printing
- **Era:** 1880s onward
- **Technique:** CMYK dots at different angles create continuous-tone images
- **Significance:** The industrial application of moiré control — printers spend their careers avoiding what moiré artists celebrate.

### Roy Lichtenstein
- **Work:** Pop art with Ben-Day dots
- **Significance:** The "error" (halftone dots) became the style. Lichtenstein weaponized the print defect.

### Sigmar Polke
- **Work:** "Rasterbild" series
- **Significance:** Halftone dots as painterly material — the fine art of print interference.

## Anamorphosis

### Hans Holbein the Younger
- **Work:** "The Ambassadors" (1533)
- **Technique:** Skull anamorphosis in the foreground
- **Significance:** The original anamorphic secret — hidden image visible only from one angle.

### Leonardo da Vinci
- **Work:** Early anamorphic experiments in notebooks
- **Significance:** The Renaissance interest in perspective distortion.

### Baroque Ceiling Painting
- **Technique:** Trompe-l'œil that only works from the church entrance
- **Significance:** The architectural anamorphosis tradition — the building as observer-dependent art.

### Pepper's Ghost
- **Era:** 19th century
- **Technique:** Stage illusion using reflection
- **Significance:** Reflection-based anamorphosis — the theatrical ancestor of moiré magnification.

## Op Art & Perceptual Art

### Victor Vasarely
- **Work:** "Vega" series, "Zebra" series
- **Significance:** The "father" of Op Art. Systematic exploration of visual perception.

### Bridget Riley
- **Work:** "Fall" (1963), "Movement in Squares" (1961)
- **Significance:** The fine art ancestor of digital moiré. Her paintings are "visual beats" in pigment.

### Richard Anuszkiewicz
- **Work:** "Temple of the Radiant Yellow" (1963)
- **Significance:** Color interaction as primary subject — the chromatic moiré ancestor.

### Josef Albers
- **Work:** "Homage to the Square" series
- **Significance:** Color interaction studies — the theoretical foundation of chromatic moiré.

## Video & Electronic Art

### Nam June Paik
- **Work:** "TV Buddha" (1974), "Global Groove" (1973)
- **Technique:** Video feedback, television as art medium
- **Significance:** The feedback loop as subject — the ancestor of temporal feedback moiré.

### Steina and Woody Vasulka
- **Work:** Video synthesizer art
- **Technique:** Analog video processing, feedback systems
- **Significance:** The video art pioneers — feedback as generative method.

### Bill Viola
- **Work:** "The Reflecting Pool" (1977), "The Passing" (1991)
- **Technique:** Slow-motion temporal manipulation
- **Significance:** Memory made visible — the ancestor of temporal moiré.

### Woody Vasulka
- **Work:** "The Art of Memory" (1987)
- **Significance:** Video as memory system — the conceptual ancestor of temporal feedback moiré.

## Music & Rhythm

### Terry Riley
- **Work:** "In C" (1964)
- **Technique:** Phasing patterns — musical modules drifting out of sync
- **Significance:** The audio equivalent of temporal moiré.

### Steve Reich
- **Work:** "It's Gonna Rain" (1965), "Come Out" (1966)
- **Technique:** Tape loops phasing against each other
- **Significance:** The musical beat frequency — the auditory ancestor of visual moiré.

### Brian Eno
- **Work:** Ambient music, generative systems
- **Significance:** Systems that generate music — the musical equivalent of generative moiré systems.

## Digital Predecessors

### Early Computer Graphics
- **Era:** 1960s-70s
- **Pioneers:** John Whitney Sr., Lillian Schwartz, Stan VanDerBeek
- **Significance:** The first digital moiré — interference patterns from early CRT displays and plotters.

### John Whitney Sr.
- **Work:** "Catalog" (1961), "Permutations" (1966)
- **Technique:** Analog computer animation with moiré-like interference
- **Significance:** The grandfather of digital generative art.

### Demoscene
- **Era:** 1980s-present
- **Significance:** Real-time graphics programming as art form. Many demos use moiré-like interference patterns.

### Shadertoy
- **Era:** 2013-present
- **Founder:** Inigo Quilez, Pol Jeremias
- **Significance:** The platform that democratized shader art — including moiré.
