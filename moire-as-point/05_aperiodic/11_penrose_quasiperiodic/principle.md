# Penrose / Quasiperiodic Moiré — Moiré That Never Repeats

## The Phenomenon

Two Penrose tilings overlaid with slight translation. No true periodicity = no true repeating moiré. You get *local* interference that mutates as you move — moiré that *evolves* rather than cycles.

## Why It Never Repeats

Penrose tilings have **quasiperiodic** order — long-range order without translational symmetry. The pattern is self-similar (contains copies of itself at different scales) but never exactly repeats.

When two such tilings interfere:
- The beat frequencies are *irrational* ratios (related to the golden ratio φ ≈ 1.618)
- The moiré pattern is also quasiperiodic
- No matter how far you move, you never see the exact same interference twice

## The Golden Ratio Connection

Penrose tilings are built from two rhombus shapes with angles related to 36° and 72° — multiples of π/5. The quasiperiodicity comes from the fact that 2π/5 is an irrational multiple of π.

In moiré terms:
- If grid A has frequency `f`
- And grid B has frequency `f * φ`
- The beat frequency is `f * (φ - 1) = f / φ` (since φ² = φ + 1)

This irrational ratio means the moiré never completes a cycle. It's always "almost" repeating but never quite.

## The Ammann Bars

Penrose tilings contain "Ammann bars" — parallel lines that run through the tiling at five different angles. These bars are *almost* periodic — they have long/short spacings in Fibonacci sequences.

When two Penrose tilings interfere, the moiré forms along these Ammann bars. The fringes have the same Fibonacci long/short rhythm — a moiré that "breathes" in golden ratio proportions.

## Five-Fold Symmetry

Unlike square (4-fold) or hexagonal (6-fold) moiré, Penrose moiré has **5-fold symmetry**. The interference patterns have pentagonal, decagonal, and star-shaped zones. This symmetry is "forbidden" in crystals — which is why quasicrystals were controversial when discovered.
