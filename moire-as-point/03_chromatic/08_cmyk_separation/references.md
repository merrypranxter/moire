# References

## Artists
- **Roy Lichtenstein** — Ben-Day dots as primary aesthetic. The pop art halftone.
- **Sigmar Polke** — "Rasterbild" series. Halftone dots as painterly material.
- **Cristina Ghetti** — "CMYK" series. Deconstructed halftone as geometric art.
- **Richard Hamilton** — "Just what is it that makes today's homes so different, so appealing?" (1956). Collage with halftone elements.

## Scientific
- **Halftone Mathematics** — The AM (amplitude modulated) screening used in printing is exactly moiré generation, optimized to be invisible.
- **Rosette Formation** — The 4-color rosette is a moiré pattern that printers carefully control. Misalignment creates "moiré" — the print defect.
- **Dot Gain** — Physical ink spread that changes effective dot size. Critical for accurate moiré simulation.
- **FM Screening** — Frequency modulated (stochastic) screening eliminates ordered moiré but creates grain moiré.

## Historical
- **Benjamin Day** — 19th century printing technique using dots of uniform size. The origin of Ben-Day dots.
- **Lichtenstein's Sources** — He copied actual comic book panels, including their halftone errors. The "error" became the style.
- **1970s Color Separation** — Pre-digital era. Color separations were hand-cut rubylith films. Registration was physical and imperfect — moiré was a constant battle.
